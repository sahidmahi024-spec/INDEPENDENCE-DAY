History of Independence Day and the Indian Flag

India celebrates its Independence Day on August 15, marking the end of nearly two centuries of British colonial rule in 1947. The freedom struggle, led by figures like Mahatma Gandhi, Jawaharlal Nehru, and Subhas Chandra Bose, involved civil disobedience and non-violent resistance, culminating in the Indian Independence Act of 1947. On this day, the Prime Minister hoists the national flag at the Red Fort in Delhi, followed by a parade and a speech, symbolizing national pride and unity.

The Indian national flag, known as the Tiranga, is a horizontal tricolor of saffron (courage and sacrifice), white (peace and truth), and green (fertility and growth), with the navy-blue Ashoka Chakra, a 24-spoke wheel symbolizing progress and righteousness, at its center. The flag was officially adopted on July 22, 1947, by the Constituent Assembly, just weeks before independence.

The flag's evolution began in 1906 with the Calcutta Flag, featuring green, yellow, and red stripes with lotuses, "Vande Mataram," and a crescent moon and sun. In 1907, Madam Bhikaji Cama hoisted a modified version in Stuttgart, Germany, the first Indian flag raised abroad. The Home Rule Movement’s 1917 flag included red and green stripes with the Union Jack and Saptarishi stars. In 1921, Pingali Venkayya designed a flag with red and green stripes, later adding a white stripe and charkha at Mahatma Gandhi’s suggestion. By 1931, the Congress adopted a saffron-white-green tricolor with a charkha. In 1947, the charkha was replaced with the Ashoka Chakra, proposed by Surayya Tyabji, to create the modern Tiranga.
